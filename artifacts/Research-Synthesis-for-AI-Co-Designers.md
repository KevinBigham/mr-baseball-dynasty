## 1. Executive summary

Across the papers, the big design lesson is simple:

A game’s opening should not merely explain controls or show spectacle. It should quickly make the player feel four things:
1. **I know what I can do.**
2. **I can do one important thing successfully.**
3. **There is more depth here than I fully understand yet.**
4. **I care what happens next.**

The research converges on a practical rule:

**The first session is a promise.**
It should combine clarity, agency, competence, curiosity, and a glimpse of future payoff.

If an opener is clear but boring, players bounce.
If it is flashy but confusing, players bounce.
If it is rich but controlling, players feel managed instead of invited.
If it is mysterious but unrewarding, curiosity curdles into distrust.

The strongest openings therefore do five jobs at once:
- give a clear immediate goal
- teach through action, not lecture
- preserve autonomy
- create one early “aha” mental-model shift
- tease future depth or meaning

## 2. The shared doctrine for all three games

### Principle 1: Design the opening around a **promise**, not just a tutorial
Players do not stay only because the first minutes are “fun.” They stay because the opener hints at deeper systems, future power, unfolding story, or interesting dilemmas.

**Design question:** What is the one thing the player should want by minute 5?

Examples:
- a star prospect they want to draft
- a cap-space or roster puzzle they want to solve
- a forbidden area, boss, or weird truth they want to reach

### Principle 2: Make the first goal crystal clear
The player should understand the immediate objective in one sentence.

Good opening goals:
- “Make one meaningful franchise decision.”
- “Win this one short scenario.”
- “Get through this one dangerous room.”

Bad opening goals:
- diffuse multi-step chores
- unclear dashboards
- lore with no action anchor
- multiple equally urgent demands at once

### Principle 3: Teach by **doing**, not by preloading information
The papers are ruthless about this: long front-loaded instruction is fragile.
People learn better when they act, see consequences, and then receive targeted guidance.

Use this sequence:
1. let the player try
2. let them succeed or fail safely
3. clarify why it worked or failed
4. only then layer on the next idea

### Principle 4: Match tutorial weight to complexity
Complex games need more onboarding support.
Simple or legible games benefit more from experimentation.

This matters a lot for your portfolio:
- the Dynasty games are likely system-dense and need stronger scaffolding
- Cactus Ed likely benefits from shorter, more embedded teaching

### Principle 5: Preserve autonomy like it is sacred
Players want help, but they do not want to feel puppeteered.
The best support is structured without being overbearing.

A strong default pattern:
- first hint nudges
- second hint clarifies
- third hint solves

Never begin with the solve.

### Principle 6: Build an early **mental-model shift**
Novices begin with surface impressions.
The best early design reveals the deeper logic underneath the surface.

This is the “ohhh, that’s how this game thinks” moment.

Examples:
- the draft board is not just ratings; it is uncertainty management
- the salary cap is not just money; it is long-term flexibility and timing
- the platforming level is not just jumping; it is rhythm, baiting, and world-reading
- a press conference is not just flavor text; it shapes relationships and future arcs

### Principle 7: Satisfy autonomy, competence, and relatedness early
Across the motivation papers, the same three needs keep showing up:
- **Autonomy:** I can choose
- **Competence:** I can improve and be effective
- **Relatedness:** I care about someone, something, or some larger context

If the opening satisfies all three, players are much more likely to continue.

### Principle 8: Treat immersion as layered, not magical
Immersion is not an on/off switch.
The first session usually needs to reach **engagement** and **engrossment** before anything like “total immersion” becomes plausible.

So do not chase transcendence immediately.
Chase:
- low friction
- meaningful focus
- emotional pull
- control fluency

### Principle 9: Plant curiosity deliberately
Curiosity is not one thing. The papers point to at least several useful kinds:
- curiosity about place
- curiosity about systems
- curiosity about other people/factions/characters
- curiosity about future rewards
- curiosity about hidden or out-of-place things
- curiosity about uncertainty and strategy

Every opener should plant more than one curiosity hook.

### Principle 10: Reward curiosity when players follow it
If you point at a thing and imply it matters, then it must pay off.
Not always with loot, but with *something*:
- information
- power
- advantage
- surprise
- emotional payoff
- world texture
- a shortcut
- a better decision context

A world that repeatedly fails to answer curiosity teaches players not to care.

### Principle 11: Timing is a teacher
Players learn temporal patterns even when they cannot articulate them.
Cadence teaches.
Rhythm teaches.
Spacing teaches.

Use timing intentionally in:
- enemy wind-ups
- menu feedback
- commentary pacing
- sim reveal cadence
- reward timing
- interruption timing
- button response and animation timing

### Principle 12: Test first-session behavior scientifically
Do not ask only “did they like it?”
Track:
- time to first meaningful action
- time to first success
- time to first confusion spike
- hint use
- quit point
- replay/continue intent
- where curiosity produced exploration vs where confusion produced wandering

## 3. What this means for each game

## Mr Football Dynasty

### Working assumption
This is a deep, browser-based, narrative-driven football franchise management simulator with draft war room decisions, salary cap management, AI trades, coaching systems, owner dynamics, broadcast commentary, rivalry arcs, and dynasty legacy tracking.

### Best research-backed opener shape
The opening should **sell the fantasy of being a football GM** before it tries to teach the full machine.

#### What the first 10–15 minutes should accomplish
- establish the player’s role: “You are the GM, not a passive menu operator.”
- present one clear immediate goal
- give one fast success
- reveal one deeper systems insight
- tease one long-horizon dynasty consequence

#### Strong opening candidate
A tightly scripted “franchise crisis” start:
- owner gives one mandate
- roster has one obvious weakness
- player makes one draft/trade/cap decision
- player sees one immediate consequence
- player gets one broadcast/press/reaction beat that makes the world feel alive

This opener teaches through a meaningful decision instead of a sterile menu tour.

#### Specific design advice
1. **Teach the league through dilemmas, not encyclopedias.**
   Instead of dumping rules, stage small, consequential choices.

2. **Gate complex systems behind immediate purpose.**
   Do not introduce every cap mechanic, contract wrinkle, scouting nuance, and coaching subsystem at once.
   Start with the one concept needed for the current decision.

3. **Create an early mental-model shift.**
   For example: “Ratings are not truth; they are filtered by scouting uncertainty and context.”

4. **Use story to deepen management, not decorate it.**
   Press conferences, owner messages, and rivalry beats should change player interpretation of decisions.

5. **Preserve agency in support tools.**
   If AI assistance is present, it should suggest, compare, and explain—not auto-pilot the franchise.

6. **Use future hooks.**
   Tease a future rival, a risky prospect, a looming cap crunch, or a coach whose scheme could redefine the roster.

#### What the AI collaborator should watch for
- too many systems before first success
- menus that ask for expert knowledge before the player has context
- narrative text that does not change decisions
- onboarding that explains but does not dramatize

## Mr Baseball Dynasty

### Working assumption
This is a sabermetrically grounded baseball franchise simulator with 162-game seasons, 30 teams, deep farm systems, scouting fog-of-war, arbitration, free agency, advanced stats, dynamic news, press conferences, rivalry systems, and owner pressure.

### Best research-backed opener shape
The opening should make the player feel that baseball knowledge is being transformed into **judgment**, not buried under jargon.

#### What the first 10–15 minutes should accomplish
- establish the player’s role as franchise decision-maker
- turn sabermetrics into understandable leverage
- create one “aha” around uncertainty, value, or projection
- make the simulation feel alive, not spreadsheet-dead

#### Strong opening candidate
A “hard baseball choice” start:
- one flawed MLB roster spot or one prospect decision
- one conflict between traditional stats and better metrics
- one owner or budget constraint
- one immediate roster move or arbitration decision
- one news/feed/press reaction that makes the league respond

This makes the player *use* baseball reasoning instead of merely reading about systems.

#### Specific design advice
1. **Stage statistical complexity.**
   Do not throw every metric at the player immediately.
   Introduce a small number of metrics in context, tied to a real decision.

2. **Teach via contradiction.**
   The best early mental-model shift may be something like:
   “The batting average hero is not always the best asset.”
   or
   “The roster problem is not talent alone; it is cost, timeline, and fit.”

3. **Use curiosity around hidden futures.**
   Baseball is wonderful for future-reward curiosity:
   prospects, player development, arbitration cliffs, injury risk, breakout variance, and trade windows.

4. **Make the world answer the player.**
   News feed, rivalry commentary, morale changes, and owner patience should react clearly to choices.

5. **Use optional help that explains reasoning.**
   If AI or advisor support exists, it should expose *why* a suggestion is strong.
   The player must learn decision logic, not just copy recommendations.

6. **Keep the first victory small but real.**
   Example: turning one weak spot into one better roster configuration or winning one compact scenario because of a better decision.

#### What the AI collaborator should watch for
- stats introduced without decision context
- too many comparable numbers with no hierarchy
- early complexity that feels like homework
- narrative systems that do not alter incentives or emotions

## Cactus Ed's Happiest Place

### Working assumption
This is a browser-based psychedelic action-platformer with absurdist satire, 30+ levels, bosses, mini-games, procedural visuals, hand-coded movement/combat, conspiracy narrative layers, fourth-wall breaks, and commercial interruptions.

### Best research-backed opener shape
This game should likely teach through **lived experience, rhythm, surprise, and curiosity magnets**.

#### What the first 10–15 minutes should accomplish
- make movement and attack feel good fast
- establish the tone immediately
- create one early mastery moment
- create one deeper world mystery
- show that weirdness is not random; it is meaningful

#### Strong opening candidate
A short playable sequence where:
- Ed moves, jumps, and attacks within seconds
- the player safely learns one movement-combat rhythm
- one bizarre interruption or conspiracy clue appears
- one optional curiosity path rewards exploration
- one enemy or boss-like encounter teaches timing and pattern reading

#### Specific design advice
1. **Keep explicit tutorial text extremely light.**
   This kind of game likely benefits from embedded, interactive learning more than lecture.

2. **Use curiosity magnets in the first space.**
   Apply the research patterns directly:
   - extreme point
   - visual obstruction
   - out-of-place element
   - suspicious spatial connection

3. **Reward optional exploration quickly.**
   If the player follows something odd, answer it with a joke, reveal, collectible, cat interaction, or world clue.

4. **Use timing as communication.**
   Enemy tells, platform windows, punch/kick cadence, interruption pacing, and boss wind-ups should quietly teach expectation.

5. **Turn weirdness into coherence.**
   The player should feel, “I do not fully understand this world, but it clearly has a logic.”
   That is the sweet spot between confusion and intrigue.

6. **Make onboarding feel like the game itself.**
   No classroom mode if possible.
   The tutorial should already feel like satire, comedy, danger, and personality.

7. **Use one early mental-model shift.**
   Example: “Commercial interruptions are not just jokes; they are part of the threat system / story system / world logic.”

#### What the AI collaborator should watch for
- overly long text before action
- weirdness that does not pay off
- platforming or combat timing that feels arbitrary
- inconsistent signaling that turns curiosity into confusion

## 4. Title-specific guidance for AI collaborators

When your AI collaborator proposes a feature, scene, tutorial, or system change, it should answer these six questions:

1. **What player fantasy is this reinforcing?**
2. **What immediate goal will the player understand?**
3. **What action will the player physically or cognitively perform in the first session?**
4. **What mental-model shift is this trying to create?**
5. **What curiosity hook or future payoff is being planted?**
6. **How will we know if it worked?**

A useful internal format for all design suggestions:
- Problem
- Target player state
- Proposed change
- Why this fits the research
- Predicted effect on onboarding / engagement / curiosity / agency
- What to instrument in playtests

---

## 5. What not to let the AI collaborators do

Do **not** let them:
- recommend giant up-front info dumps just because a system is complex
- optimize for “clarity” by killing mystery
- optimize for “mystery” by hiding basic readability
- use AI assistance to remove player thinking
- add worldbuilding that does not affect decisions or interpretation
- confuse menu density with strategic depth
- confuse chaos with surprise
